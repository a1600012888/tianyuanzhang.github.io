Analyzing Physical Impacts using Transient Surface Wave Imaging | CVPR 2023

Tianyuan Zhang, Mark Sheinin, Dorian Chan, Mark Rau, Matthew O'Toole, and Srinivas Narasimhan



paper

Perceive the enviroments from vibrations

Sense of hearing enables human to perceive the enviroment beyound our line-of-sight. Using camera to measure the vibration of objects in the enviroment provides richer and stronger cues to perceive the enviroment.


We propose a novel transient surface wave imaging to analyze impact beyond our line-of-sight. We demonstrate the capbility of transient surface imaging in following demos. We image the vibration on the bottom surface of the ping-pong table to localize the strike of the ball.

We image the vibration on the floor to localize the footsteps.

Steady-state vibration and transient vibration

When two solid objects come into contact, the impact creat transient vibration waves propagates through the objects. As these waves interact with the object's properties and boundary conditions, certain frequencies got amplified and others got attenuated, resulting in the vibration modes associated with the objects' natural frequencies. Prior work has been using cameras to measure the vibration modes of objects, and use that to recover the material properties.

This project focus on a different type of vibration signals: transient vibrations. What does transient vibration look like? We visualize the transient propagation of vibrations waves on an isotropic plastiboard immediatelly after being knocked at the center of the board below:

From the above videos, we realize that the transient vibration is highly structured, and we can easily infer the impact location from the video. However, this video is recorded using a LDV (laser doppler vibrometer), repeated over 8000 times. What if we can have a sensor that can directly measure the tiny vibrations at multiple points?

Explain the methods

We use cameras from [cite], which measures tiny vibrations (2D surface tilts) at a sparse set of points with 63K HZ. The surface tilts are converted to surface wave propagating direction.

The measured direction can be used to triangulate the impact location. We use backprojection to reconstruct a heatmap of the predicted impact location.

Towards infering impact force and properties of impacting objects

Can we go a step-further from impact localization to infering more information about the impact? like impact force and properties of impacting objects? We provide a proof-of-concept experiment below.

We drop the ball at different height towards the same point on the table. We can see the maximual magnitude of the transient vibration is square-root of the falling height. While as indicated in [cite], the maximal impact force is also square-root of the falling height. Which means there is potentially a linear relationship between the the magnitude of the transient vibration and the impact force.

Extending current localization capbilities

By using transient vibration to study impacts, we can achieve applications that are not impossible before with cameras, like localizing the physical impact outside the field of view of cameras. our method constitutes a new class of transient vibration imaging, and can open the door for novel vision tasks.
